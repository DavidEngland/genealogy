# Family Information for Welch-185
A Historical Narrative of Major Nicholas Welch, Loyalist

1.0 Introduction: The Life of a Revolutionary War Loyalist

The American Revolution was a civil war as much as a war for independence, a conflict that divided communities, friends, and families. To fully comprehend this complex era, it is essential to move beyond the monolithic Patriot-versus-British narrative and examine the lives of individuals who chose to remain loyal to the Crown. The story of Major Nicholas Welch offers a compelling case study of a colonial American who sided with the British. Reconstructed from fragmented genealogical data, military accounts, and contradictory civic records, his life provides a window into the Loyalist experience. From his origins in colonial Pennsylvania to his military service in the Carolinas and his contested final resting place, Welch's biography illustrates the personal allegiances and regional conflicts that defined the Revolutionary period. This narrative will begin by examining the documented origins and family background that shaped his early life.

2.0 Origins and Ancestry

Genealogical records are the foundational elements for reconstructing a historical life, establishing an individual's place within the societal and familial structures of their time. For Nicholas Welch, these records place his beginnings firmly in colonial Pennsylvania, a generation before the revolutionary turmoil that would define his adult life.

The consensus from surviving records indicates that Nicholas Welch was born in Lancaster County, Pennsylvania. While the exact date varies slightly across sources, it is consistently placed between 1738 and 1740. Family records identify his parents as John Welch and Elizabeth Whiteside, who were married on February 6, 1738, in Quittapahilla, Pennsylvania. This upbringing in a well-established colonial region provided the backdrop for the pivotal role he would later play on the opposite side of the Patriot cause during the American Revolution.

3.0 Military Service for the Crown

The southern colonies became a critical and brutal theater of the Revolutionary War, where the British military actively recruited colonists who remained loyal to King George III. The military career of Nicholas Welch provides a specific and vivid example of this Loyalist mobilization. He was not a British regular but a colonist who took up arms for the Crown, demonstrating the deep divisions within American society.

According to military and historical records, Welch served between 1778 and 1780, attaining the rank of Major in the Royal British Army of North Carolina. His role was not limited to the battlefield; he was an active and persuasive recruiter for the Loyalist cause. An account of his efforts highlights his methods and the arguments he used to sway his neighbors:

"Nicholas Welch, who had lived just above Moore on Indian Creek, went from this vicinity eighteen months prior to this and joined the British army. He appeared dressed in a new uniform and exhibiting a considerable quantity of gold coins, representing himself as Major of Hamilton's Regiment. He urged the men to embody at once, telling of the fall of Charleston, Buford's defeat, and the bad condition of affairs for the Americans everywhere. By his narratives and judicious use of his guineas he prevailed over Moore and it was determined to organize at once."

His self-identification as an officer in "Hamilton's Regiment" is a significant detail, grounding his activities within a known Loyalist unit and adding valuable specificity to his service. This recruitment effort culminated in his participation in the Battle of Ramsour's Mill on June 20, 1780, a fierce engagement fought entirely between Loyalist and Patriot militias. Having explored his public role in the conflict, the narrative now turns to the records of his private and family life.

4.0 Marriages and Family

Family records are indispensable for building a complete biographical portrait, offering insights into personal relationships, lineage, and the domestic sphere that existed alongside public life. The available genealogical data outlines Nicholas Welch's documented marriages and the extensive family he raised, while also presenting a chronological puzzle requiring careful analysis.

Records show that Nicholas Welch married Elizabeth E. Moore (1740-1799) in North Carolina in the year 1757. However, a separate entry from Philadelphia documents a marriage between a "Nicholas Welsh" and a Sarah Farren on December 26, 1794. This later marriage date precedes the documented death of his first wife, Elizabeth, by five years, creating a chronological discrepancy. Such inconsistencies are common in genealogical research and often point to complex life events that can only be clarified by later, corroborating evidence.

5.0 Final Years and Contested Death Records

Conflicting information is a frequent obstacle in genealogical research, requiring careful analysis of all available evidence to establish the most probable account. The case of Major Nicholas Welch's death is a prime example of this challenge, as the source file itself presents two contradictory death locations. A close examination of primary source documents, however, not only resolves this conflict but also solves the matrimonial puzzle presented in the previous section.

The primary genealogical record for Nicholas Welch lists his death in Rapides Parish, Louisiana, between 1810 and 1820. However, a source citation within that very same record, along with other compelling evidence, points to an alternate and more strongly supported location: Giles County, Tennessee. The claim of a Louisiana death is immediately questionable, as Welch’s last known residence was in Tennessee. As one researcher notes, "Why would he have been in Louisiana?" A systematic evaluation of the evidence overwhelmingly substantiates the Tennessee location.

1. Tax Records: Official records establish Welch's presence in Tennessee in the years leading up to his death. He is explicitly listed on the 1812 Tax List for Giles County, Tennessee, making it his last documented place of residence.
2. Probate Records: The most definitive evidence comes from the Giles County Court Minutes. On December 8, 1814, the court ordered that "letters of adm. of est. of Nicholas WELCH, dec'd, issue to Sarah WELCH," and in a subsequent order, appointed commissioners to allot provisions for "Sarah WELCH, widow of the late Nicholas WELCH."

This official probate record is the key that unlocks the final chapter of Welch's life. It provides a legal record of his death in Giles County by that date and, crucially, identifies his surviving widow as "Sarah." This fact corroborates the 1794 Philadelphia marriage record to Sarah Farren and resolves the seeming chronological conflict. It demonstrates that Welch did indeed remarry, and it is his second wife, Sarah, who administered his estate in Tennessee.

While the Louisiana record persists in some aggregated databases, the primary source evidence from tax and probate records in Giles County provides a definitive location and timeframe for Nicholas Welch's death, identifying his second wife, Sarah, as his surviving widow.

6.0 Conclusion: Reconstructing a Loyalist Life

The life of Major Nicholas Welch is a compelling journey from a colonial subject in Pennsylvania to a committed Loyalist field officer in the Carolinas and, finally, a family patriarch in frontier Tennessee. His story is a testament to the power of genealogical and historical records to illuminate the lives of those who are often overlooked in grand national histories. Piecing together his biography from birth registers, military accounts, and court minutes reveals a man of conviction whose choices placed him on the losing side of a revolutionary conflict.

At the same time, his story underscores the critical importance of careful analysis. His biography serves as an exemplary case study in resolving documentary conflict. The seemingly unrelated marriage certificate from Philadelphia and the probate minute from Giles County, when synthesized, create a coherent narrative that clarifies both his second marriage and the true location of his death. The work of a historian or genealogist is not merely to collect facts, but to weigh evidence, connect disparate sources, and construct the most probable account of the past. Through this process, a figure like Major Welch emerges not as a simple footnote, but as a complex individual whose life enriches our understanding of a divided America.

Based on the sources, **Lewis Welch** was a central figure in the early settlement of **Perry County, Tennessee**, and served as a bridge between his father's Loyalist past and his descendants' integration into the American Republic.

### **Connection to Nicholas Welch**

Lewis Welch was the **son of Major Nicholas Welch** (c. 1738–1814) and his second wife, **Elizabeth E. Moore** (1740–1799). His father was a prominent Loyalist officer who fought for the British at the Battle of Ramsour’s Mill. In contrast to his father’s British service, Lewis Welch is documented as a veteran of the **War of 1812**, fighting **for the United States**.

### **Presence in Perry County, Tennessee**

Lewis Welch migrated to the Tennessee frontier following a path from North Carolina through South Carolina and Kentucky.

* **1820 Census:** Lewis is recorded in the **Perry County census**. His household at this time included:
  * Known sons **John C. Welch** and **Elijah Welch**.
  * One unknown son.
  * Three females, one of whom is believed to be his wife, whose identity remains a research gap.
* **Family Network:** He was part of an expansive Welch family presence in the area. His likely brother, **Nicholas Welch Jr.** (born 1786), was one of the vanguard settlers on **White Oak Creek** in Perry County between 1818 and 1820 and later served as the **Sheriff of Perry County** after 1830. Another relative, **Zachariah Welch**, held a War of 1812 land grant near the Wayne/Perry County line.

### **Migration and Legacy**

* **Prior Residence:** Lewis was in South Carolina around 1800 (where his son John C. was born) and moved to **Hopkins County, Kentucky**, by 1810, where he lived next to Henry Welch.
* **Lineage to Serena Welch:** Through his son **Elijah C. Welch** (1810–1867), Lewis is the grandfather of **Serena Welch** (c. 1833–1884). Serena’s marriage to James Monroe Hargrove connected the Welch line to the family of Patriot Revolutionary veteran John Washington Hargrove Sr., symbolizing the reconciliation of former enemy families on the frontier.

### **Recommended Records for Further Research**

While the identity of Lewis's wife (Elijah's mother) is unknown, the sources suggest searching **Hopkins County, Kentucky land deeds** (1806–1820) to look for dower rights signatures that might name her. Additionally, **Hardin County, Tennessee** records for his son John C. Welch may contain biographical clues regarding the family's matriarch.
